Biomonitoring and Community Science on the Sunshine Coast

Introduction

The Sunshine Coast in British Columbia is an 180 km stretch of rugged maritime landscape along the mainland's southwest coast, nestled between Howe Sound and Desolation Sound. It includes or overlaps with the unceded territories of the Sḵwx̱wú7mesh, shíshálh, Tla'amin, Klahoose, and Homalco First Nations.

The Sunshine Coast features a narrow coastal lowland flanked by mountains, deep fjords such as Jervis Inlet, and a rugged, island dotted shoreline fronting the Strait of Georgia.

The region supports rich biodiversity anchored by Coastal Western Hemlock and Coastal Douglas fir forests, old growth stands, wetlands, estuaries, and marine waters. Terrestrial habitats host black bears, cougars, elk, wolves, coyotes, black tailed deer, bald eagles, marbled murrelets, and many other species. Coastal and marine ecosystems include kelp forests, rare glass sponge reefs, salmon streams, and tide pools that are home to molluscs, sea anemones, and fish. Resident and Bigg's orcas, seals, sea lions, and migratory birds rely on these productive waters and shorelines.

Paired with its extraordinary biodiversity, the Sunshine Coast has an unusual number of formal and informal non profit organizations whose primary purpose is to conserve, enhance, and steward biodiversity. These organizations are often involved in biomonitoring programs and in education about biodiversity on the coast.

Volunteer and Education Opportunities

The primary purpose of this document is to introduce those Sunshine Coast non profit organizations that offer opportunities for community members to become involved in biomonitoring as volunteers or to take advantage of the educational opportunities they provide. We focus on opportunities within the Sunshine Coast Regional District, extending from Langdale to Egmont.

Organizations

The Loon Foundation

"The Loon Foundation was founded in 2002 as the Ruby Lake Lagoon Nature Reserve Society," and since that time, "our scope has expanded well beyond the Ruby Lake Lagoon, and in 2020 we changed our name to The Loon Foundation to better represent our wide reaching efforts to conserve biodiversity and foster environmental stewardship." In addition to community science and education, and in collaboration with the shíshálh Nation, this organization is leading the build of the Pender Harbour Ocean Discovery Station.

Biomonitoring Programs:
  • Forage fish
  • Seagrass
  • Invasive species
  • Pinnipeds
  • Salmonid escapement
  • Juvenile escapement
  • Larval crabs
  • Coastal water nearshore birds
  • Loons

The Foundation also organizes an annual BioBlitz with findings recorded on iNaturalist.

Education: Occasional educational gatherings and a summer camp for children typically occur in any given year.
Volunteer Opportunities: Yes. Contact the organization to determine current opportunities.
Website: https://www.loonfoundation.org/
Location: Madeira Park
Contact: info@loonfoundation.org

Pender Harbour Wildlife Society

"Founded in 1979, the Pender Harbour and District Wildlife Society has sought to reverse the damage caused by human habitation and improve the life of flora and fauna across the length of the Sunshine Coast."

Biomonitoring: The Society organizes the Pender Harbour Annual Christmas Bird Count, one of two Christmas bird counts in the Sunshine Coast Regional District.
Education: Occasional educational gatherings, including guest speakers, occur in any given year.
Volunteer Opportunities: Contact the Society to determine current opportunities in their other conservation programs.
Website: https://penderharbourwildlife.com/
Location: Madeira Park

Sargeant Bay Society

"Established in 1978, the Sargeant Bay Society is a volunteer non profit organization located on the Sunshine Coast of British Columbia, Canada. We work to support, enhance, and protect the ecologically sensitive habitats found within Sargeant Bay, its watershed, and neighbouring wetlands."

Biomonitoring:
  • Colvin Creek spawning counts by Sargeant Bay's Streamkeeper
Education: Occasional educational gatherings and nature walks occur in any given year.
Volunteer Opportunities: Yes. Contact the Society to determine current opportunities.

Sunshine Coast Friends of Forage Fish

"Our group collects spawning data on the beaches of the Sunshine Coast, British Columbia. By sifting small beach sediment samples, we hope to find forage fish eggs, a sure sign of a positive spawning beach."

Biomonitoring: Bi-monthly forage fish monitoring in Gibsons and Davis Bay.
Education: The group often holds educational sessions about forage fish at community events.
Volunteer Opportunities: Yes, volunteers are engaged in the sampling conducted by this group.
Website: https://friendsofforagefish.com/
Location: Sechelt
Contact: info@friendsofforagefish.com or diannesanford@gmail.com

Sunshine Coast Natural History Society

"Although originally set up to manage Sechelt Marsh, the Sunshine Coast Natural History Society now caters for a wide range of natural history interests on the Sunshine Coast of BC." There is a focus on birds; however, interests span natural history, including bats, dragonflies, and butterflies.

Biomonitoring: The Society organizes the Sunshine Coast Annual Christmas Bird Count, one of two Christmas bird counts in the Sunshine Coast Regional District.
Education: Meetings include discussion of recent natural history sightings. Other educational events include nature walks and regular invited speakers.
Website: https://sites.google.com/view/sunshinecoastnhs/home
Location: Sechelt
Contact: webmaster.scnhs@gmail.com

"The Sunshine Coast Salmonid Enhancement Society is a charitable organization dedicated to sustaining and building salmon stocks in local waterways and facilitating public education regarding salmonid habitat and life cycles."

Volunteer Opportunities: Opportunities to be involved with hatchery work, including fin clipping, fish transfers, fish releases, spawning, incubation work, and fish feeding.

Sunshine Coast Wildlife Project

"We are a small group of biologists working to conserve the Sunshine Coast's precious natural heritage for generations to come. Since 2006, we have been working with community partners to help conserve wildlife and their habitats."

Biomonitoring: The Project is involved in a variety of biomonitoring projects, including those centred on goshawks, turtles, snakes, amphibians, bats and owls, often in collaboration with university researchers.
Education: The Project provides workshops, seminars and youth programs centred on biodiversity and conservation.
Volunteer Opportunities: Yes, several of their programs engage volunteers.

Location: Sechelt

Additional Opportunities

There are also broader, regional and national platforms where community members can contribute to biomonitoring and community science:

  • BC Nature (https://www.bcnature.ca/): A provincial umbrella organization for naturalist clubs; members can participate in surveys, field trips, and species at risk monitoring across the Sunshine Coast.
  • eBird – Sunshine Coast (https://ebird.org/region/CABC): A global bird observation platform that allows users to submit bird sightings specific to the Sunshine Coast region, contributing to long term population and distribution data.
  • iNaturalist – Sunshine Coast (https://www.inaturalist.org/places/sunshine-coast): A community science platform where users can upload photos of plants, animals, and fungi observed on the Sunshine Coast; observations help document local biodiversity and are often used by researchers and conservation groups.
  • Birds Canada (https://www.birdscanada.org/you-can-help/citizen-science): Birds Canada offers diverse volunteer community science opportunities ranging from monitoring nest success and counting birds in winter to surveying nocturnal species and mapping breeding populations.
