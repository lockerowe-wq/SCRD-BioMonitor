On Being a Biomonitor

Getting Involved

There are many ways to contribute to biomonitoring on the Sunshine Coast.

Volunteer

The non-profit organizations featured on this website offer a variety of volunteer opportunities for community members to contribute to biodiversity monitoring. Whether you're a seasoned naturalist or just starting out, young or not so young, there are likely programs suited to your interests and experience level. Reach out to any of the organizations featured on this site to find out how you might get involved.

Record your Observations

You can also contribute by engaging on your own with easy to use web-based platforms, such as eBird and iNaturalist, where you can record your observation of species you've seen at home or any place you might be on the Coast.

Join or Donate

Finally, you can contribute by joining or donating to one or more of the non-profits featured here.

Impact

In addition to the fun of engaging with biodiversity and like-minded people, your observations will contribute to our understanding of the state of biodiversity on the Coast, and even to new policy and formal research in ecology and evolution. Some of these impacts will be shared on the Research Stories page.

Community Biomonitors

Here we will feature some of the Sunshine Coast community members leading or volunteering in some of the local biomonitoring programs. More to come!

Jenn Blancard has been volunteering in biomonitoring projects on the Sunshine Coast for almost 10 years. Over the course of these years, Jenn has volunteered counting bats, weeding turtle beaches, monitoring turtle hatchings, completing amphibian surveys, conducting larval Dungeness crab monitoring (including population genetics studies), and helping collect data for the Salish Sea Marine Survival Project. Recently, she has started doing water quality monitoring with the Hotel Lake Advisory Group. For work, she has played a central role in 11 marine coastal waters monitoring programs and one freshwater monitoring program through the Loon Foundation.

Asked if she had a favourite, Jenn responded, "They are all my favourite. If I had to choose the top 17, I may be able to narrow it down. Although I get really excited when we get cephalopods, which tend to be in the juvenile salmon survey or the light trap survey."

A final thought: "I have heard in the past that people are afraid to volunteer when the word biology or science is involved, as they don't feel like they have the skill set. I would love to challenge everyone to shed that assumption and come out to participate. You will learn that there are jobs for all: from handling organisms, to educating the public, to recording the numbers, to filling buckets of water to refresh tanks. Many hands make light work. And there is a lot of work to do."
